01 · Most common

Phishing Attacks

Phishing is when an attacker pretends to be a trusted organisation — a bank, delivery company, government body or even a friend — to trick you into clicking a link, downloading a file, or handing over login details, OTPs or card numbers.

It arrives as email, SMS ("smishing"), WhatsApp messages, voice calls ("vishing") or fake websites that look almost identical to the real thing.

Red flags to spot instantly

  • Urgency or threats — "act within 24 hours or your account will be blocked"
  • Mismatched or misspelled domains — arnaz0n-support.com instead of amazon.in
  • Generic greetings — "Dear Customer" instead of your real name
  • Requests for OTP, PIN, CVV or passwords — no legitimate bank ever asks for these
  • Links that don't match the text shown (hover before clicking)
  • Unexpected attachments, especially .exe, .zip or .apk files
Example message
SBI
SBI-Alert
Today, 11:42 AM
Dear Customer, your A/C will be BLOCKED today due to KYC expiry. Update immediately: http://sbi-kyc-update.in/verify

Failure to update will suspend all services.
1Creates panic with "BLOCKED today" — real banks give weeks of notice.
2The link domain is sbi-kyc-update.in — not onlinesbi.sbi.
3Uses HTTP, not HTTPS — official banking sites are always secured.

How to protect yourself

→Never click links in unexpected messages — go to the official app or type the website URL yourself.
→Enable 2-factor authentication on email, banking and social media accounts.
→Verify by calling the organisation's official number — found on their real website or your bank card, not the message.
Example scenario
📱
"Wrong" payment received
You get a notification: "₹5,000 received from Ramesh K." Minutes later, "Ramesh" calls, says it was a mistake, and asks you to refund via a QR code he sends.
🔲
The QR code is a trap
Scanning a QR code and entering your UPI PIN to "receive" money actually authorises an outgoing payment — UPI PINs are only ever needed to send money, never to receive it.
💸
Money leaves your account
The "refund" QR actually debits your account for whatever amount the scammer set — often far more than ₹5,000.
02 · Financial

UPI & Banking Frauds

UPI's speed and simplicity is also what scammers exploit. Common patterns include fake "refund" QR codes, screen-sharing apps disguised as customer support tools, fake customer care numbers found via search, and SIM-swap attacks.

Golden rule

You NEVER need to enter a UPI PIN, scan a QR code, or share an OTP to receive money. PIN and OTP are only for sending or authorising payments.

Other common traps

  • Fake bank "customer care" numbers found on Google or social media
  • Remote access apps (AnyDesk, TeamViewer) requested by "support staff"
  • SMS claiming a debit card is expiring, with a link to "re-verify"
  • Investment or trading apps promising guaranteed high returns
  • Loan apps that demand an upfront "processing fee"

How to protect yourself

→Only use official bank apps or numbers printed on your card/passbook for support.
→Set a daily UPI transaction limit and enable transaction alerts via SMS and email.
→Never install remote-access or screen-sharing apps for someone claiming to be "support."
03 · Long-term risk

Identity Theft

Identity theft happens when someone collects enough of your personal information — Aadhaar number, PAN, date of birth, address, photos — to impersonate you for loans, SIM cards, or fraudulent accounts.

It often starts small: a "lucky draw" form asking for Aadhaar details, a fake KYC update call, or photos of documents shared casually on WhatsApp groups.

How attackers collect your data

  • Fake contests, surveys or "verify to win" forms
  • Public Wi-Fi networks that intercept unencrypted traffic
  • Data breaches at companies that store your details
  • Oversharing documents (Aadhaar, PAN photos) over chat
  • Social media posts revealing date of birth, mother's name, pet names — common security answers
Real-world signs your identity may be compromised
!A loan or credit card you never applied for appears on your CIBIL report.
!You receive OTPs for logins or transactions you didn't initiate.
!A new SIM is activated on your number without your request.
!Friends report receiving strange messages "from you" on social media.
!Your Aadhaar shows authentication attempts you didn't make (checkable on UIDAI's portal).

How to protect yourself

→Use Aadhaar masking (mAadhaar app) when sharing copies — it hides all but the last 4 digits.
→Check your credit report (CIBIL/Experian) every few months for unfamiliar accounts.
→Lock your Aadhaar biometrics via UIDAI when not actively needed.
The pressure playbook
AAuthority: "This is Inspector Sharma from Cyber Cell, your Aadhaar is linked to a money laundering case."
UUrgency: "Your account will be permanently closed in 30 minutes unless you act now."
FFear: "A digital arrest warrant has been issued — stay on this video call or face arrest."
GGreed: "You've won a lottery — pay a small processing fee to claim ₹25,00,000."
TTrust: A cloned voice or hacked account of a friend asking to "urgently transfer money."
04 · Psychological

Social Engineering

Social engineering is the human side of hacking — instead of breaking software, attackers manipulate people using urgency, authority, fear, and trust to bypass logic.

The newest and most dangerous variant in India is the "digital arrest" scam, where fraudsters impersonate police or CBI officers on video calls, claiming the victim is under investigation and must transfer funds for "verification" — a real-time, high-pressure con designed to prevent victims from thinking clearly or consulting anyone.

The one rule that defeats all of these

Pause. No genuine authority — police, bank, or government — will ever ask for money, OTPs, or "verification transfers" over a call or video call. Hang up, and verify independently using official numbers.

05 · Targets students

Fake Job & Internship Scams

These scams specifically target students and fresh graduates with offers that sound exciting but follow a predictable pattern designed to extract money or personal data.

Common formats

  • Task-based scams: "Like 5 videos, earn ₹500" — early small payouts build trust, then larger "investments" are demanded before withdrawal
  • Pay-to-get-hired: Offer letters that require a "security deposit," "training fee," or "laptop charge" before joining
  • Data harvesting forms: "Apply now" forms asking for bank details, Aadhaar, and PAN for a job that doesn't exist
  • Work-from-home data entry: Requires purchasing a "starter kit" or software license
Example message
HR
+91 98XXX-XXXXX
WhatsApp · Today
🎉 Congratulations! You've been shortlisted for a Part-Time Online Job with [Big Company]. Salary ₹35,000/month. To confirm your slot, pay a refundable registration fee of ₹999 via the link below.

bit.ly/job-confirm-2026
1Real recruiters never ask candidates to pay anything — ever.
2Shortened links (bit.ly) hide the real destination.
3"Refundable" fees are almost never refunded.

How to protect yourself

→Verify the company's official careers page and email domain directly — not links sent to you.
→Legitimate employers never ask for payment, deposits, or your bank PIN/OTP at any hiring stage.
→Search the company name + "scam" before engaging further.
Forms of online harassment
1Trolling & hate comments: Repeated abusive or threatening messages on posts.
2Impersonation: Fake profiles created using your photos and name.
3Sextortion: Threats to share private images unless money or favours are given.
4Doxxing: Sharing someone's private information (address, number) publicly to incite harassment.
5Cyberstalking: Persistent monitoring or messaging across platforms despite being blocked.
06 · Online safety

Cyberbullying & Online Harassment

Online harassment can affect anyone, but students and young people are especially vulnerable. It's never something to "just ignore" — Indian law treats many of these acts as criminal offences under the IT Act and IPC/BNS provisions.

What to do if it happens to you or someone you know

  • Don't respond or retaliate — this often escalates the situation
  • Document everything: screenshots with timestamps, usernames, and URLs
  • Use platform reporting tools — Instagram, WhatsApp and Facebook all have dedicated harassment reporting flows
  • Block the account across all platforms
  • Report to the National Cyber Crime Portal (cybercrime.gov.in) — has a dedicated section for women/child safety
  • Talk to a trusted adult, teacher, or counsellor — you are not alone in this
07 · Technical

Malware & Ransomware

Malware is software designed to damage, spy on, or take control of your device. Ransomware is a specific type that locks your files and demands payment to unlock them.

How infections typically start

  • Downloading apps from outside the Play Store / App Store (APK files via WhatsApp)
  • Clicking pop-up ads claiming "Your device is infected — click to clean"
  • Plugging in unknown USB drives
  • Opening email attachments from unknown senders (especially .exe, .scr, .js)
  • Pirated software or "cracked" app downloads
  • Fake "screen recording" or "remote support" apps sent during scam calls
Warning signs your device is infected
⚠Battery drains unusually fast or phone overheats while idle.
⚠Unfamiliar apps appear, or settings change without your action.
⚠Excessive pop-up ads even outside the browser.
⚠Files suddenly become inaccessible with a ".locked" extension and a ransom note.
⚠Friends receive spam messages or links sent "from your account."

How to protect yourself

→Only install apps from official stores, and check permissions requested at install time.
→Keep your OS and apps updated — updates patch security holes attackers exploit.
→Back up important files regularly to cloud storage or an external drive disconnected from your device.
